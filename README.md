## Overview

Bhoot (Sanskrit bhūta, from a root meaning 'past' or 'that which has been') is the most widespread South Asian term for a restless ghost. In Hindu tradition, a bhoot is the spirit of a deceased person who has not progressed to the afterlife, typically because of a violent or untimely death, unfulfilled obligations, or improper funeral rites. Bhoots are said to be recognisable by certain distinctive signs: backward-facing feet, casting no shadow, speaking with a nasal voice, floating just above the ground (which is sacred), and a preference for white clothing and certain trees. Sub-categories include the churel (a woman who dies in childbirth), the pret (hungry ghost), and the pishacha (carnivorous flesh-eating spirit). Methods of warding off bhoots in folk practice include water, iron or steel objects, the smell of burnt turmeric, sprinkling earth on oneself, and invoking deities or holy figures. In Hindu practice, the appropriate response is not destruction but atma-shanti, ritual peace-giving derived from the Atharva Veda, intended to allow the spirit to be reborn or to complete unfinished business. Bhoot legends are deeply embedded in modern South Asian language, literature, cinema, and television — from the Bengali Thakurmar Jhuli stories to numerous Bollywood horror films.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Sanskrit bhūta, past participle of the root √bhū ('to be, to become'), meaning 'that which has been' or 'past existence'.
- **Meaning**: 'The past one'; one who once was; a ghost or spirit. The same root underlies the elements (pañca-bhūta) in Hindu cosmology, but in folk usage 'bhoot' refers specifically to the restless dead.
- **First use**: Vedic Sanskrit (c. 1500–500 BCE); the term has continuous textual use across classical and medieval Sanskrit literature.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Restless souls awaiting moksha** _(Hindu tradition (puranic and tantric))_

A bhoot is the soul of a person whose attachments, violent death, or improperly performed funerary rites prevent its progression to rebirth or final liberation; the appropriate intervention is atma-shanti rather than destruction.

**Djinn / spirit overlap (Muslim South Asia)** _(Folk Islamic practice)_

In South Asian Muslim folk practice, bhoot beliefs overlap with Islamic djinn lore; the two categories are not always neatly separated in Indian, Pakistani, and Bangladeshi villages.

### Scientific

**Sleep paralysis and grief** _(Cross-cultural sleep-paralysis researchers (Adler, Jalal et al.))_

Many bhoot encounters fit the cross-cultural pattern of sleep paralysis hallucinations and bereavement-related apparition experiences.

**Social functions of ghost belief** _(Beth Roy, Diane Mines, Sudhir Kakar)_

Anthropologists treat bhoot beliefs as performing social work: enforcing kinship duty (correct funerary rites), policing the boundaries of caste and gender (especially in churel narratives about women's deaths), and articulating community history.

### Skeptical

**Folkloric and cultural construct** _(Narendra Dabholkar (FIRA / Andhashraddha Nirmoolan Samiti), Indian Rationalist Association)_

Mainstream Indian rationalist groups (Andhashraddha Nirmoolan Samiti, FIRA) treat bhoot belief as a sometimes-harmful superstition, particularly when used as a cover for exploitative 'exorcisms' targeting women.
